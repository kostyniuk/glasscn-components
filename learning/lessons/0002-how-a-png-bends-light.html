The problem it solves

Frosted glass is easy: backdrop-filter: blur() saturate(). But Apple's Liquid Glass is a lens — near the rim of every pill, the background visibly bends, like light crossing curved glass. Look at your own macOS Control Center screenshot: wallpaper streaks kink as they pass under a pill's edge.

No CSS function can do that, because blur and friends only ever answer "what color should this pixel be, averaged from its neighborhood?" Bending needs a different power: "paint this pixel using the backdrop from somewhere else." Exactly one machine in the SVG toolbox has it.

The idea: a picture used as instructions

That machine is feDisplacementMap. It takes two inputs: in (the pixels to bend — for us, the backdrop) and in2 (a second image called the displacement map). The map is never shown. It's read as instructions: for each pixel, the map's Red value says how far to reach sideways for the sample, and Green says how far up/down:

sample position = (x + scale·(R/255 − 0.5),  y + scale·(G/255 − 0.5))

Unpack that slowly — it's the only formula in this series:

  • R and G are 0–255, so R/255 − 0.5 runs from −0.5 to +0.5. 128 lands on ≈0: "don't move". That's why displacement maps look gray — gray is "do nothing".
  • scale (an attribute on the machine) converts that −0.5…+0.5 into pixels. Max reach = scale/2. Your component's refraction prop is literally this attribute.
  • So a map is a grid of little arrows wearing a PNG costume: gray = no arrow, redder = arrow pointing right, greener = arrow pointing down.

Your component's job, then, is just to paint the right arrows: a JS loop fills a <canvas> with gray in the middle and outward-pointing arrows in a band along the edge (strong at the rim, fading inward), encodes it as a PNG data-URL, and loads it into the filter with feImage — a machine whose only job is "load this image and put it on the line as a result". Because the whole thing runs in backdrop-filter, the bent pixels come from the live page behind the glass — drag it anywhere and the lens stays honest, for free.

The window model: where the effect actually lives

A natural misreading (worth killing early): "we apply the glass effect to the component." We don't — and we couldn't. The component's own pixels are almost nothing: an 8% white tint, a hairline border, the rim ring, your children. The glass you see is the page behind, repainted through the assembly line, clipped to the element's outline. The element is a window; the filter applies to what you see through it — like a pane of shower glass, which is itself nearly invisible and "looks like" mostly the distorted room behind it.

The browser's paint order when it reaches the pill:

  1. The page behind is already painted.
  2. Snapshot the rectangle under the pill.
  3. Run the snapshot through the line: displacement bends, blur() frosts, saturate() pops.
  4. Paint the result back, clipped to the pill's rounded shape.
  5. Paint the element's own stuff on top: tint, border, children (unfiltered — that's why labels stay crisp), rim ring.

This is also why it must be backdrop-filter and not plain filter: with filter: url(#lens) the line would receive the element's own pixels — your button label would warp while the page behind stayed straight. The exact opposite of glass.

Check: when you drag the pill, which of the five steps changes?

Only what steps 1–2 see — the snapshot content. The map, the filter, and the element's own pixels are all untouched. That's why dragging is free, and it's the same fact as practice experiment 1 below.

Gray = "don't move". The colored rim is the arrows: red-ish reaches right, green-ish reaches down — always pointing out of the shape.

Three experiments, in order:

  1. Drag the pill over the text. The bending follows it with zero recomputation — the map encodes the pill's shape, not what's behind it.
  2. Slide refraction to 0, then 120. Only the scale attribute changes — same PNG, longer arrows. At 120 you'll also see the rim pixelate: a real limitation, noted below.
  3. Tick "show the map", then move the bezel slider. The gray interior and colored band ARE the instructions. Notice bezel changes do rebuild the PNG — geometry changes are the expensive ones.

Boundaries

  • Chromium-only when combined with backdrop-filter. Safari and Firefox parse backdrop-filter: url() but don't render it — and CSS.supports() wrongly says they do (mdn/browser-compat-data #24110). That's why liquid-glass.tsx sniffs the user agent and falls back to frost. The gate + fallback design deserves its own lesson.
  • No supersampling. The machine reads one instruction-pixel per output pixel; at high scale the rim pixelates (you saw it at 120). No fix exists inside this primitive — buttery high-distortion effects are WebGL territory (your liquid-switch's technique).
  • The arrows have to come from somewhere. How the canvas loop knows the distance and direction to the nearest edge of a rounded rectangle — the SDF math — is the next lesson.

Tie-in to your mission

This is the heart of components/ui/glasscn/liquid-glass.tsx: createDisplacementMap() paints the arrows (128 + dir · magnitude · 127 is the encode step), feImage loads them, scale={refraction} is the prop you expose, and the cache key width:height:radius:bezel is why quiz 4's support ticket happens.
