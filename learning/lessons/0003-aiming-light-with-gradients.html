Aiming Light with Gradients

← 0002 · How a PNG Bends Light Glossary Reference: pipeline cheat sheet
Where this sits. Lessons 0001–0002 covered the refraction layer (the SVG displacement map). This lesson is a different, simpler layer that sits on top: the rim — the thin bright line tracing the edge of the pill. In liquid-glass.tsx it's the <span aria-hidden> overlay. We just changed its look; this lesson is exactly that change.
Given for today (not taught here)
The mask + mask-composite trick that carves the overlay down to a 1.5 px frame. Treat it as a black box: "it confines whatever I paint to a thin ring." It earns its own lesson — ask me when you want it.

The problem it solves

A flat ring of one color doesn't read as glass. Real glass has a bevelled edge: the parts of the rim that face a light source catch a bright specular streak; the parts that face away fall into shadow. Your eye reads that light/dark split as thickness and curvature — without it the pill looks like a sticker, not a lens.

So the rim can't be a solid border. Its colour has to change depending on which way that stretch of edge is pointing. The old code did this with one diagonal gradient — "light coming from the upper left" — which lit the top-left and bottom-right corners. The iOS 26 material in your screenshot does something different: bright along the top and bottom, dark down the sides. The task is to redirect the light.

The idea: a gradient is a function of one axis

The one thing to internalise: a linear-gradient assigns colour by position along a single axis, and ignores the other completely.

linear-gradient(to bottom, …) is a function of y only. Every pixel at the same height gets the same colour, no matter its x. Lay that over the ring and the top edge and bottom edge (the horizontal runs of the frame) pick up the colours at the ends of the gradient; the left/right runs just fade through it vertically. So a white-at-both-ends vertical gradient = lit top and bottom.

That alone leaves the sides limp, not dark. Fix it by stacking a second gradient on a different axis. background takes a comma-separated list, painted front-to-back — first one wins. Add linear-gradient(to right, …) (a function of x only) that's dark at both ends, list it first, and it paints shadow onto the left and right edges, over the white. Two one-axis gradients, two pairs of edges: 

background:
  linear-gradient(to right,  rgba(0,0,0,.55), rgba(0,0,0,0) 16%, rgba(0,0,0,0) 84%, rgba(0,0,0,.55)),
  linear-gradient(to bottom, rgba(255,255,255,.7), rgba(255,255,255,0) 30%, rgba(255,255,255,0) 70%, rgba(255,255,255,.6));

The percentage stops are where the work happens: colour at 0% and 100%, fully transparent from 16%–84% (or 30%–70%). That pins each colour to the extreme edge and leaves the middle clear — so light hugs the very top/bottom and shadow hugs the very left/right, instead of washing across the whole pill. Widen those transparent bands and the highlight tightens; narrow them and it spreads.

Practice

Toggle between the old and new rim and watch where the light sits. Then switch to Custom and drag the stops — the live CSS underneath is exactly what you'd paste into the overlay's style. Flip Plain backdrop to judge the rim without the busy background fighting it.

1. You apply only linear-gradient(to bottom, white, transparent 50%, white) to the rim. Which edges light up?

2. With both gradients in place, what actually makes the sides dark — given the white gradient is already lighting top and bottom?

3. At the corners both gradients overlap. To make the shadow win there, where must the to right (dark) gradient go in the background list?

Boundaries

Layered linear gradients are the right tool when the highlight follows straight axes — top/bottom, left/right. They stop being right when:

  • The light should sweep around the curve (a single source glinting continuously round a circle). That's a conic-gradient, not stacked linear ones.
  • You need the ring itself — its thickness, why mask-composite carves a frame out of a filled box. That's the masking lesson (ask me).
  • The bend of the background through the glass — that's refraction, the SVG displacement map from lesson 0002, a different layer entirely. The rim is paint on the glass; refraction is distortion through it.

Why this matters for your mission

Your mission is to own every layer of LiquidGlass well enough to tune and defend it. The rim overlay is one of the four triage layers you listed — and the one most exposed to taste. When a registry consumer says "the highlight looks wrong on my dark theme," this is the layer, and now you can reason about it directly: the highlight position is set by which axis each gradient runs on, its intensity by the colour stops, its tightness by the transparent band. You changed liquid-glass.tsx by hand this session; this lesson is the model that makes that change a decision rather than a guess.
